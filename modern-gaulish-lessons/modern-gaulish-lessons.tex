%\subsection{Menghavan 1: Bréthré – Aman Dhathach:  Gweranúé Donach
%\subsubsection{Lesson 1: Verbs – Present Tense: Personal Pronouns
%\noindent In the first lesson you will learn how to put a verb in the present tense and how to use it with a personal pronoun.
%<ol>
%<li><strong> Verbs in the present tense</strong></li>
%</ol>
%\noindent Each verb has a basic form or root form, known as a “verbal noun”. It has the same function as the infinitve in English.
%\noindent Verbal nouns can end in a consonant, in -i, -a, -e, and in just one case in -ó, never in -u. Examples are below:
%\noindent ápis: to see
%\noindent men: to think
%\noindent gwel: to want
%\noindent gar: to call/
%\noindent carni: to build
%\noindent argha: to shine
%\noindent delghe: to hold
%\noindent ávó: to do, to make
%\noindent berwi: to boil
%\noindent gní: to know
%\noindent To form the present tense of these verbs an –a is added to the verbal noun in the following ways. Note that vowels in modern Gaulish can be either long or short. Vowel length changes with emphasis. The emphasis is always on the second last syllable. When words are extended the emphasis shifts accordingly.
%\noindent Verbs on a consonant:
%\noindent ápis &gt; apísa
%\noindent men &gt; ména
%\noindent gwel &gt; gwéla
%\noindent gar &gt; gára
%\noindent Verbs on –i:
%\noindent carni &gt; carna
%\noindent Verbs on –a:
%\noindent argha &gt; argha &#8211; nothing changes
%\noindent Verbs on -e:
%\noindent delghe &gt; delgha
%\noindent Verbs on -ó exchange the -ó for an -a:
%\noindent ávó &gt; áva
%\noindent Verbs on -wi retain the final -i:
%\noindent berwi &gt; berwía
%\noindent Verbs on -i where the -i is the only vowel retain the -i:
%\noindent gní &gt; gnía

%<h2><span style="color:#ff0000;"><strong>Lesson 2: Personal Pronouns As Object</strong></span></h2>
%\noindent In the second lesson you will learn how to use a pronoun when it is the object of a phrase with a verb.
%<ol>
%<li><strong> Personal pronouns as object of an active verb</strong></li>
%</ol>
%\noindent The object of a sentence is the thing to which something is being done. It is the receiving end of the action performed by the verb.
%\noindent When personal pronouns are the object of a sentence they can take two different forms. The first form is identical to the form they take when they are the subject of a sentence, except for one. The subject of a sentence is the giving end of the action performed by the verb.
%<table>

%<td width="308">personal pronouns as subject</td>

%\noindent ti: you
%\noindent é: he
%\noindent í: she
%\noindent í: it
%\noindent ni: we
%\noindent sú: you pl.
%\noindent sí: they</td>
%<td width="308">mi: me
%\noindent ti: you
%\noindent é: him
%\noindent í: her
%\noindent í: it
%\noindent ni: us
%\noindent sú: you pl.
%\noindent ís: them</td>
%</tr>

%\noindent Only the third person plural pronoun differs: ís instead of sí.
%\noindent These pronouns are used when they are the receiving end of an active verb. An active verb is a verb that performs the main action of a phrase. It will have a subject which will be performing the action. It will be in a form that indicates the time and the way the action is being performed.
%\noindent V = verb
%\noindent S = subject
%\noindent O = object
%\noindent In modern Gaulish a phrase has the standard order Verb-Subject-Order. It is an aspect that is characteristic of the Celtic languages and is not common in English.
%\noindent Using the verbs that were introduced in lesson 1 we can construct examples:
%\noindent apísa mi: I see &gt; apísa mi ti: I see you
%\noindent In the phrase “apísa mi ti” the verb “apísa” comes first, the subject “mi” comes second, and the object “ti” comes third. This is indicated like this:
%\noindent apísa mi ti
%\noindent V      S   O
%\noindent We can see that the verb “apísa” is an active verb because it is in the present tense: it has the present tense ending –a.
%\noindent Here are more examples:
%\noindent gára í mi: she calls me
%\noindent delgha é ni: he holds us
%\noindent gnía sí sú: they know you (pl.)
%\noindent When the object pronoun starts with a vowel, such as é, í and ís, and they follow a subject pronoun, that object pronoun receives an extra letter ch- at the start. This letter ch is pronounced like the –ch in the Scottish word “loch”.
%\noindent apísa mi chí: I see her
%\noindent ména mi chí: I think it
%\noindent gwéla í ché: she wants him
%\noindent áva é chí: he does it
%\noindent gnía sú chís: you (pl.) know them
%\noindent gára í chís: she calls them

%<li><strong> Personal pronouns as object of a verbal noun</strong></li>
%</ol>
%\noindent The verbal noun is the basic root form of the verb, called infinitive in English.
%\noindent It is easiest to think of the verbal noun of modern Gaulish as the –ing form of the English verb.
%\noindent E.g. can: to sing &gt; can: “singing”
%\noindent cána mi chí: I sing it
%\noindent When the personal pronouns are the object of a verbal noun, they take on a different form:
%\noindent mi &gt; imí
%\noindent ti &gt; ithí
%\noindent é &gt; iché
%\noindent í &gt; ichí
%\noindent ni &gt; iní
%\noindent sú &gt; isú
%\noindent ís &gt; ichís
%\noindent When a verbal noun is used in a phrase with an active verb it comes immediately after the subject:
%\noindent gwéla mi can: I want to sing
%\noindent In this phrase the verbal noun is the object of the active verb:
%\noindent gwéla mi can
%\noindent V        S    O
%\noindent If we think of the verbal noun as the –ing form of the verb, we could literally translate this as:
%\noindent want I singing (&gt; “I want singing”)
%\noindent V      S   O
%\noindent If we use a personal pronoun to be the object of the verbal noun we use the special form described above:
%\noindent gwéla mi can ichí: I want to sing it
%\noindent In this phrase the two words “can ichí” become the new object of the phrase.
%\noindent gwéla mi can ichí
%\noindent V       S    [O       ]
%\noindent The above phrase can be literally translated as “I want singing of-it”.
%\noindent The particle i- that the pronouns are attached to indicates possession of something:
%\noindent imí: of-me
%\noindent ithí: of-you
%\noindent iché: of-him
%\noindent ichí: of-her
%\noindent ichí: of-it
%\noindent iní: of-us
%\noindent isú: of-you pl.
%\noindent ichís: of-them
%\noindent The phrase “can ichí” translates as “singing of-it”. If we add an imaginary definite article [the] to the English version it makes sense:
%\noindent can ichí: [the] singing of-it &gt; gwéla mi can ichí: I want [the] singing of-it

%</ol>
%\noindent In lesson 2 we saw that when a pronoun was used as an object it had a special possession particle i-. This particle is not used with anything else, only with the pronoun. When we use a noun we just replace the pronoun and the particle with a noun:
%\noindent cána mi cánu: I sing a song
%\noindent gwéla mi can ichí: I want to sing it
%\noindent gwéla mi can cánu: I want to sing a song
%\noindent The phrase “can cánu” means “[the] singing of a song”. The English word [the] is not used.
%\noindent This phrase has two nouns: 1. the verbal noun “can”. 2. the noun “cánu”. In this phrase the first noun “can” is possessed by the second noun “cánu”. In English this is indicated by the word “of”. In modern Gaulish this is indicated by the position of the word: the second word possesses the first word.

%\noindent e.g.:
%\noindent in gwir: the man
%\noindent in ép: the horse
%\noindent in mapath: the boy
%\noindent in curu: the beer
%\noindent in pen: the head
%\noindent in duvr: the water
%\noindent The article “in” can be used in cases of possession. It can only be used with the second noun, which is the one possessing the first noun. The first noun can never have the article in front of it.
%\noindent e.g.:
%\noindent cun gwir: a dog of a man [a man’s dog]
%\noindent cun in gwir: a dog of the man [the man’s dog]
%\noindent The English phrase between brackets [&#8230;] shows a very good translation of the modern Gaulish phrase. It only uses one article and can only ever use one article. It is not possible to say “the man’s the dog”.
%\noindent The second noun possesses the first noun. The second noun is the only noun that can have the article.

%<h2><strong> <span style="color:#ff0000;">Lesson 4: Gender Of Nouns</span> </strong></h2>
%\noindent In the fourth lesson you will learn how to determine the gender of nouns.
%\noindent <strong>Gender Of Nouns</strong>
%\noindent In modern Gaulish all nouns have a gender, which is either masculine or feminine. If the meaning of the noun indicates a gender, then that noun is that gender:
%\noindent gwir: man
%\noindent ben: woman
%\noindent mapath: boy
%\noindent geneth: girl
%\noindent map: son
%\noindent dúithir: daughter
%\noindent áther: father
%\noindent máthir: mother
%\noindent moth: penis
%\noindent tuthu: vagina
%\noindent If the meaning of a noun does not indicate a gender, its gender is determined by the last vowel. If the last vowel is /a/ or /i/ the noun is feminine.
%\noindent lam: hand &gt; fem.
%\noindent bis: finger &gt; fem
%\noindent tír: land &gt; fem
%\noindent cnam: bone &gt; fem
%\noindent If the last vowel is /e/, /o/ or /u/ the noun is masculine.
%\noindent pen: head &gt; masc.
%\noindent mór: sea &gt; masc.
%\noindent cánu: song &gt; masc.
%\noindent tráieth: foot: masc.
%\noindent coch: leg: masc.
%\noindent curu: beer &gt; masc.
%\noindent The –i in the diphthongs –ái-, -éi-, -ói- and –úi- is not a vowel, it is a semi-consonant, like /y/ in English. It does not count as a vowel, and its presence does not make a noun’s gender feminine:
%\noindent brói: country &gt; last vowel is /o/, the –i is the semi-consonant &gt; masc.
%\noindent mái: place, plain &gt; last vowel is /a/ &gt; fem.
%\noindent téi: house &gt; last vowel is /e/ &gt; masc.
%\noindent gwólúith: strain &gt; last vowel is /u/ &gt; masc.
%\noindent Some nouns end in a double consonant where the last consonant is l, n or r. When pronounced there is a dull indistinct sound between the second last consonant and the l, n or r. This sound is called schwa, and is represented by the symbol [?]. It is not considered a vowel and is not written. It does not affect the gender of a noun. The gender of such a noun is determined by the last vowel before the schwa:
%\noindent sédhl: seat &gt; last vowel is /e/ &gt; masc.
%\noindent sparn: thorn &gt; last vowel is /a/ &gt; fem
%\noindent livr: book &gt; last vowel is /i/ &gt; fem.
%\noindent Some nouns end in a diphthong followed by a double consonant where the last consonant is l, n or r. The gender of these nouns is determined by the last vowel before the –i of the diphthong:
%\noindent anéithl: protection &gt; last vowel is /e/ &gt; masc.
%\noindent lúithr: struggle &gt; last vowel is /u/ &gt; masc.
%\noindent bóithl: hit &gt; last vowel is /o/ &gt; masc.
%\noindent amáithl: service &gt; last vowel is /a/ &gt; fem.
%\noindent Nouns of animals are masculine by default, even if the vowels are /a/ or /i/:
%\noindent garan: heron &gt; masc.
%\noindent cun: dog &gt; masc.
%\noindent lóern: fox &gt; masc.
%\noindent ép: horse &gt; masc.
%\noindent caval: [draught] horse &gt; masc.
%\noindent bó: cow [generic name for cattle]
%\noindent These nouns can be made feminine by adding the suffix –is:
%\noindent garanis: female heron
%\noindent cunis: bitch
%\noindent lóernis: vixen
%\noindent épis: mare (also casich)
%\noindent cavalis: female draught horse
%\noindent Nouns indicating human functions or activities are also masculine by default:
%\noindent drúidh: scholar &gt; masc.
%\noindent gwerchovreth: magistrate &gt; masc.
%\noindent tiern: boss, chief &gt; masc.
%\noindent dan: official, manager &gt; masc.
%\noindent These nouns can also be made feminine by adding the suffix –is:
%\noindent drúidhis: female scholar
%\noindent gwerchovrethis: female magistrate
%\noindent tiernis: female boss, chief
%\noindent danis: female official, manager

%<h2><strong><span style="color:#ff0000;">Lesson 5: Plural Formation</span> </strong></h2>
%\noindent In the fifth lesson you will learn how to form the plural of nouns.
%\noindent <strong>Plural Of Nouns</strong>
%\noindent The plural of the most nouns is formed by adding the plural suffix –é to the noun.
%\noindent gwir &gt; gwir + -é
%\noindent mapath &gt; mapath + -é
%\noindent Because it is an open vowel it causes the emphasis to shift one place closer to the end of the word. Where the plural ending is not separated from the previous syllable by more than one consonant the vowel of that syllable before the ending –é becomes long as well as emphasised.
%\noindent gwir &gt; gwíré (men)
%\noindent mapath &gt; mapáthé (boys)
%\noindent geneth &gt; genéthé (girls)
%\noindent map &gt; mápé (sons)
%\noindent dúithir &gt; dúithíré (daughters)
%\noindent If the plural ending is separated from the previous syllable by more than one consonant the vowel of that sylable is short.
%\noindent ethn: bird &gt; ethné: birds
%\noindent carch: rock &gt; carché: rocks
%\noindent If a word ends in a vowel the ending –é follows immediately after that vowel, making that vowel emphasised and long.
%\noindent cánu: song &gt; canúé: songs
%\noindent Most plurals in Galáthach are formed in this way. There are two exceptions only:
%\noindent <strong>Plural Of Woman</strong>
%\noindent The plural of the word for woman is different.
%\noindent ben: woman &gt; mná: women
%\noindent This is attested as such in Old Gaulish.
%\noindent <strong>Plural Of Natural Pairs</strong>
%\noindent The plural of things that naturally occur as pairs is formed by adding the prefix dá-, which means “two”.
%\noindent óp: eye &gt; dáóp: eyes
%\noindent coch: leg &gt; dáchoch: legs
%\noindent lam: hand &gt; dálam: hands
%\noindent For the word aus “ear” the prefix dá- becomes shortened to d-.
%\noindent aus: ear &gt; daus: ears
%\noindent In cases where these things occur in numbers other than two the normal plural suffix –é is used.
%\noindent ópé damathal: [the] eyes of a spider
%\noindent &gt; spiders have eight eyes
%\noindent coché ép: [the] legs of a horse
%\noindent &gt; horses have four legs
%\noindent ópé gwíré: the eyes of men
%\noindent &gt; several men together have more than two eyes
%\noindent In cases where things are referred to that are not of natural formation and may or may not come in pairs, the normal plural suffix –é is used.
%\noindent lamé gwepór: the hands of a clock
%\noindent &gt; a clock is not a natural creature, and there may be more than two hands on a clock, e.g. hours, minutes, seconds
%\noindent <strong>Plural Of Collectivity</strong>
%\noindent A great number of things that is commonly considered as one whole is indicated by the suffix –lói.
%\noindent gwep: word &gt; gweplói: vocabulary
%\noindent &gt; gweplóié: vocabularies
%\noindent sir: star &gt; sirlói: constellation
%\noindent &gt; sirlóié: constellations
%\noindent <strong>Plural After Numbers</strong>
%\noindent The plural is not used after numbers. Nouns stay in the singular.
%\noindent ép: a horse &gt; pethr ép: four horses
%\noindent nóith: night &gt; dech nóith: ten nights

%\noindent <strong>Feminine Nouns And The Article</strong>
%\noindent When feminine nouns are preceded by the article “in” their initial consonant changes according to regular patterns. This change is what marks the nouns as feminine.

%\noindent iar &gt; in ch’iar (chicken – the chicken)

%\noindent p &gt; b

%\noindent b &gt; v
%\noindent d &gt; dh
%\noindent g &gt; gh
%\noindent m &gt; w
%\noindent s &gt; sh if followed by a wowel
%\noindent s &gt; ‘ [nothing] if followed by a consonant
%\noindent n &gt; nh
%\noindent r &gt; rh
%\noindent l &gt; lh
%\noindent f &gt; fh

%<h2><span style="color:#ff0000;"><strong>Lesson 7: Adjectives – The Verb To Be</strong></span></h2>
%\noindent In the seventh lesson you will learn about adjectives, and how to use the verb “to be”.
%\noindent <strong>Adjectives</strong>
%\noindent An adjective is a word that describes a quality or a characteristic of a noun.
%\noindent sen: old
%\noindent ióinch: young
%\noindent sír: long
%\noindent bir: short
%\noindent már: big
%\noindent méi: small
%\noindent ardhu: high
%\noindent íth: low
%\noindent duv: black
%\noindent gwin: white
%\noindent galv: fat
%\noindent dái: good
%\noindent druch: bad
%\noindent There are different kinds of adjectives. The ones listed above are all natural adjectives. Adjectives can also be formed from other words by using suffixes and prefixes.
%\noindent <strong>The suffix -ach</strong>
%\noindent The suffix –ach can be used to make an adjective from another word, most usually a noun.
%\noindent caran: friend &gt; caranach: friendly
%\noindent carath: love &gt; caráthach: loveable
%\noindent nerth: strength &gt; nerthach: strong
%\noindent caun: owl &gt; caunach: owlish
%\noindent barn: judgement &gt; barnach: judgemental
%\noindent achaun: stone &gt; achaunach: stony
%\noindent <strong>The suffix –ch</strong>
%\noindent The suffix –ch is similar to the suffix –ach. It is used for words ending on a diphthong in –i or on –u, where –ach would be impractical.
%\noindent grau: sand &gt; grauch: sandy
%\noindent téi: house &gt; téich: domestic
%\noindent <strong>The suffix -ídhu</strong>
%\noindent The suffix –ídhu is used for words that end on –ch, to avoid doubling up of the –ch- sound.
%\noindent boch: mouth &gt; bochídhu: mouthy
%\noindent coch: leg &gt; cochidhu: leggy
%\noindent carch: rock &gt; carchídhu: rocky
%\noindent bruch: heather &gt; bruchídhu: heathery
%\noindent <strong>The suffix -in</strong>
%\noindent The suffix –in is only used to describe qualities of living creatures, people, animals etc.
%\noindent bledh: wolf &gt; bledhin: wolf-like (lupine)
%\noindent gwir: man &gt; gwirin: masculine
%\noindent ben: woman &gt; benin: feminine
%\noindent ép: horse &gt; épin: equine
%\noindent cun: dog &gt; cunin: canine
%\noindent ernu: eagle &gt; ernúin: aquiline
%\noindent <strong>The prefixes su- and du-</strong>
%\noindent The prefixes su- (good) and du- (bad) can be attached to nouns to create adjectives.
%\noindent áiedh: face &gt; su+áiedh = good looking
%\noindent &gt; du+ áiedh = ugly
%\noindent If the emphasis in these constructions is not on the vowel /u/ of the prefixes, they become sw- and dw-
%\noindent &gt; swáiedh: good looking
%\noindent &gt; dwáiedh: ugly
%\noindent <strong>Position of the adjective</strong>
%\noindent An adjective always follows the nouns it says something about.
%\noindent gwir: man &gt; gwir caranach: a friendly man
%\noindent mapath: boy &gt; mapath méi: a little boy
%\noindent cun: dog &gt; cun duv: a black dog
%\noindent ép: horse &gt; ép gwin: a white horse
%\noindent <strong>Mutation of the adjective</strong>
%\noindent If an adjective says something about a feminine noun it undergoes mutation of its initial consonant. This is how gender is indicated.
%\noindent ben: woman
%\noindent tech: beautiful
%\noindent &gt; ben dech: a beautiful woman
%\noindent épis: a mare
%\noindent áchu: fast
%\noindent &gt; épis háchu: a fast mare
%\noindent geneth: girl
%\noindent gwimp: pretty
%\noindent &gt; geneth chwimp
%\noindent The adjective does not change for the plural.
%\noindent in ghenéthé chwimp: the pretty girls
%\noindent in wná dech: the beautiful women
%\noindent in gwíré galv: the fat men
%\noindent <strong>The verb “to be”</strong>
%\noindent The verb “to be” is the only irregular verb in modern Gaulish.
%\noindent The verbal root (or infinitive) is “bis”.
%\noindent &gt; gwéla mi bis láen = I want to be happy
%\noindent The present tense form is “esi”. It does not take an –a.
%\noindent &gt; Esi mi láen = I am happy

%\noindent In the eighth lesson you will learn about adverbs.
%\noindent <strong>Adverbs</strong>
%\noindent An adverb is a word that describes a quality or a characteristic of a verbal action.
%\noindent English examples are: quickly, quietly, calmly, naturally, normally. In English they are usually formed with the suffix –ly.
%\noindent In Galáthach adverbs are formed by placing the particle “in” in front of an adjective. The adjective undergoes mutation of its initial consonant.
%\noindent áchu: quick
%\noindent in háchu: quickly
%\noindent áva mi chí: I do it

%\noindent gwolth: hair
%\noindent en: in
%\noindent iuth: soup

%\noindent a ghála mi?: can I? &gt; question.

%\noindent <strong>Question Words</strong>
%\noindent 
%\noindent pé: what, which

%\noindent pí: who

%\noindent pémái: where

%\noindent <strong>3. The Verb To Have</strong>
%\noindent 
%\noindent Galáthach does not have a specific verb “to have”. Instead it uses a compound construction using the verb “to be”. It is made of the personal pronoun of the entity that “has” something, followed by the verb “to be”. The two are connected with a hyphen.
%\noindent 
%\noindent mi-esi ép: I have a horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent &gt; mi-esi translates as “with-me, to-me” &gt; mi-esi ép = “to-me” is a horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent The conjugation is as follows:
%\noindent 
%\noindent mi-esi: I have
%\noindent ti-esi: you have
%\noindent é-esi: he has
%\noindent í-esi: she has
%\noindent ni-esi: we have
%\noindent sú-esi: you (pl.)
%\noindent sí-esi: they have
%\noindent 
%\noindent If the subject of a phrase is specified it comes first, followed by the pronoun+to-be construction.
%\noindent 
%\noindent in gwir: the man
%\noindent é-esi: he has
%\noindent cun: dog
%\noindent &gt; in gwir é-esi cun: the man has a dog
%\noindent 
%\noindent in dóné: the people
%\noindent sí-esi: they have
%\noindent gavré: goats
%\noindent &gt; in dóné sí-esi gavré: the people have goats
%\noindent 
%\noindent In a question the question particle “a” comes before the pronoun+to-be construction. It causes mutation to the first letter of the construction.
%\noindent 
%\noindent pé a di-esi: what do you have?
%\noindent péri a shú-esi épé: why do you (pl.) have horses?
%\noindent 
%\noindent In a question where the subject is specified the question particle comes between the subject and the pronoun+to-be construction.
%\noindent 
%\noindent péri in gwir a hé-esi cun: why does the man have a dog?
%\noindent péri in dóné a shí-esi gavré: why do the people have goats?

%\noindent <strong>4. Subordinate clauses</strong>
%\noindent 
%\noindent A subordinate clause is a sentence inside of another sentence. It has a verb, subject and object that are independent of the main sentence. The two are linked with the particle “o”, which translates in English as “that” or “which”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent gnía ti: you know
%\noindent o: that
%\noindent ti-esi: you have
%\noindent pen: a head
%\noindent tech: beautiful
%\noindent &gt; gnía ti <strong><u>o</u></strong> ti-esi pen tech: you know <strong><u>that </u></strong>you have a beautiful head
%\noindent 
%\noindent apísa mi: I see
%\noindent o: that
%\noindent né hesi: there is not
%\noindent curu: beer
%\noindent éth: more
%\noindent &gt; apísa mi <strong><u>o</u></strong> né hesi curu éth: I see <strong><u>that</u></strong> there is no more beer
%\noindent 
%\noindent In the subordinate clause the second sentence (“there is no more beer”) can stand alone independently from the first one (“I see”). The two are connected by the particle “o”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent If the particle “o”is followed by a word that starts with a vowel it becomes “och”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent gwídha mi: I understand
%\noindent o: that
%\noindent esi ti: you are
%\noindent lisc: tired
%\noindent &gt; gwídha mi <strong><u>och</u></strong> esi ti lisc: I understand <strong><u>that</u></strong> you are tired

%<ol>
%<li><strong>Direct Clauses</strong></li>
%</ol>
%\noindent 
%\noindent A direct clause is a part of a sentence that refers back to the subject of the previous part of the sentence.
%\noindent 
%\noindent &gt; this is the man who wants to buy a horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent The direct clause is “who wants to buy a horse”. The word “who” refers to “the man”, which was the subject of the previous part of the sentence “this is the man”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent In Galáthach there are four ways of constructing this sentence:
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>a) introducing the clause with the word “o” and not restating the subject. The word “o” means “that” or “which”. If it is followed by a word that starts with a vowel it becomes “och”.</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent 
%\noindent esi: is
%\noindent sin: this
%\noindent in gwir: the man
%\noindent o: that (which)
%\noindent gwel: to want
%\noindent prin: to buy
%\noindent in ép: the horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent &gt; esi sin in gwir o gwéla prin in ép: this is the man who wants to buy the horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent The word “o” connects the two phrases and refers to “the man”. There is no confusion because there is no other word in the second phrase that can be interpreted as a subject:
%\noindent 
%\noindent gwéla prin in ép: want buying the horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent The word “ép” can not be interpreted as a subject because it does not follow the verb immediately. In this case there is no need to restate the subject.
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>b) introducing the clause with the word “o” and restating the subject. This is necessary when there can be confusion about which is the subject of the second phrase.</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent 
%\noindent esi sin in gwir o gwéla in ép &gt; this sentence can be interpreted in two ways:
%\noindent 
%\noindent &#8211; this is the man who wants the horse
%\noindent &#8211; this is the man who the horse wants
%\noindent 
%\noindent Because the words “in ép”, “the horse” follow directly after the verb they can be interpreted as being the subject of the second verb “gwel”, “wanting”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent Therefore the subject needs to be restated with a pronoun that refers back to the subject of the first phrase. This is called using a resumptive pronoun.
%\noindent 
%\noindent esi sin in gwir o gwéla é in ép: this is the man who wants the horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent The literal translation of this sentence is “this is the man that he wants the horse”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent In reality the direct clause becomes a subordinate clause linked to the main clause with the word “o”.
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>c) linking the second clause to the first clause with the suffix –ió which gets attached to the end of the verb of the second clause.</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent 
%\noindent esi sin in gwir gwelió in ép: this is the man who wants the horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent The suffix –ió always refers back to the subject immediately in front of it. The literal translation of this sentence is something like “this is the man wanting the horse”.
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>d) linking the second clause to the first clause with the preposition “en” in front of the verb. This causes initial consonant mutation on the verb. The verb is in the root or infinitive form.</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent 
%\noindent esi sin in gwir en chwel in ép
%\noindent 
%\noindent The literal translation of this sentence is “this is the man wanting the horse”. It is the same as using the suffix –ió.
%\noindent 
%\noindent Because the verb is in the root or infinitive form there can be no confusion about what is the subject of the second clause, because a subject can never follow a verb in the root or infinitive form.
%\noindent 
%\noindent Using the preposition “en” to link a second clause to a main clause can only be done in the present tense.
%\noindent 
%\noindent 
%\noindent People choose freely from these four ways of constructing direct clauses. However, the second one (b) is the easiest one.
%\noindent 
%\noindent 
%<ol start="2">
%<li><strong> Indirect Clauses</strong></li>
%</ol>
%\noindent 
%\noindent A direct clause is a part of a sentence that refers back to the subject of the previous part of the sentence, but in an indirect way.
%\noindent 
%\noindent this is the man whose daughter wants to buy the horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent The second clause starts with “whose daughter”. The words “whose daughter” refer back to the subject of the previous clause, but the actual subject of the second clause is the daughter, not the man himself.
%\noindent 
%\noindent In Galáthach there is only one way to construct this sentence. The clause is introduced with the word “o” and the subject is stated independently. The subject becomes “his daughter”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent esi sin in gwir o gwéla ó dhúithir prin in ép
%\noindent 
%\noindent The literal translation of this sentence is “this is the man that his daughter wants to buy the horse”. As above in Direct Clauses b) the clause becomes a subordinate clause with its own independent subject, linked to the first clause with the word “o”.

%<ol>
%<li><strong> Conjunction Clauses</strong></li>
%</ol>
%\noindent 
%\noindent A conjunction clause is a phrase that is linked to a phrase before it with a conjunction, combining into one sentence. Conjunctions are:
%\noindent 
%\noindent ma: if
%\noindent gwé: or
%\noindent ach: and
%\noindent éithr: but
%\noindent sé o: what/which (“that which”)
%\noindent 
%\noindent 
%\noindent Conjunction clauses found in the conversation above:
%\noindent 
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>a) Suvron: Anghnítha mi <strong><u>ma </u></strong>chwéla ti suling canim?</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent (Suvron: I wonder <strong><u>if </u></strong>you want to dance with me?)
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>b) Marthal: A chwéla mi suling <strong><u>gwé</u></strong> né a chwéla mi?</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent (Marthal: Do I want to dance <strong><u>or</u></strong> don’t I?)
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>c) Suvron: Gwéla mi suling canith <strong><u>ach</u></strong> né a chwéla ti?</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent (Suvron: I want to dance with you <strong><u>and</u></strong> you don’t want to?)
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>d) Marthal: Gwéla ti suling canim <strong><u>éithr</u></strong> né hesi suling <strong><u>sé o</u></strong> gwéla mi avó.</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent (Marthal: You want to dance with me <strong><u>but</u></strong> dancing is not <strong><u>what</u></strong> I want to do.)
%\noindent 
%\noindent The conjunctions “what” and “which” are very important. They can be used in two ways: as a word that refers to something else and as a question.
%\noindent 
%\noindent The English words “what” and “which” that refer to something else are translated by the phrase “sé o”, which literally means “that which”. It is used in the last example above.
%\noindent 
%\noindent 
%\noindent Marthal:[&#8230;<strong><u>]</u></strong> né hesi suling <strong><u>sé o</u></strong> gwéla mi avó.
%\noindent (Marthal: [&#8230;<strong><u>]</u></strong> dancing is not <strong><u>what</u></strong> I want to do.)
%\noindent 
%\noindent In the phrase “&#8230; dancing is not what I want to do” the word “what” is translated by “sé o”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent In the English translation the word “what” can be replaced by the phrase “that which” without changing the meaning of the sentence. If that can be done you know that the word “what” is used as a conjunction, and not as a question word. If that is the case the word “what” is translated in Galáthach as “sé o”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent If the word “what” in the English translation cannot be translated by the phrase “that which”, and a question is implied in the phrase, it needs to be translated by a question word.
%\noindent 
%\noindent 
%<ol start="2">
%<li><strong> Question Word Clauses</strong></li>
%</ol>
%\noindent 
%\noindent A question word clause is a phrase that is linked to a phrase before it with a question word, combining into one sentence. Usually there is an aspect of something unknown implied in the phrase, suggesting a question. Question words are:
%\noindent 
%\noindent pí: who
%\noindent pé: what
%\noindent pé gaman: how
%\noindent pémái: where
%\noindent ponch: when
%\noindent péri: why
%\noindent pethi: how many/how much
%\noindent 
%\noindent They are followed by the question particle “a” if a question is implied, which is most of the time. In the case of “pethi” (how many) the question particle “a” comes after the specified object (how many/much of something).
%\noindent 
%\noindent If a question is not implied they are not followed by the particle “a”. The words “pémái” (where) and “ponch” (when) can be used narratively, to tell a story. In that case they don’t indicate a question and they are not followed by the particle “a”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>a) Suvron: A ghnía ti pí a hesi mi?</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent (Suvron: Do you know who I am?)
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>b) Marthal: Né ghnía mi. Cóéth, né ghnía mi pé shulingen a hesi sé.</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent (Marthal: I don’t know. Also, I don’t know what dance that is.)
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>c) Suvron: A ghnía ti pé gaman a shuling?</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent (Suvron: Do you know how to dance?)
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>d) Marthal: Né ghnía mi. Né ghnía mi diaman pémái a hádha mó dhálam, ach né ghnía mi diaman ponch a wantha mó dháthráieth.</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent (Marthal: I don’t. I never know where to put my hands, and I never know when to move my feet.)
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>e) Suvron: A ghnía ti péri a chwéla mi o sulinga ti canim?</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent (Suvron: Do you know why I want you to dance with me?)
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>f) Marthal: Né ghnía mi. A chwéla ti gní pethi báné u guru a híva mi pap dí?</li>
