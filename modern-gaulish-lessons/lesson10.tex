%<h2><span style="color:#ff0000;"><strong>Lesson 10: Possessive Pronouns – Demonstratives &#8211; Locatives</strong></span></h2>
%\noindent In the tenth lesson you will learn what possessive pronouns and demonstratives are, and how they work.
%<ol>
%<li><strong> Possessive Pronouns</strong></li>
%</ol>
%\noindent A possessive pronoun is a word that indicates possession of a thing. In Lesson 2 and 3 we learned two ways of indicating possession: by using the possession indicating particle i-, and by putting two things next to each other, with or without the article between them. Using a possessive pronoun is the third way of indicating possession.
%\noindent A possessive pronoun is a word that comes before an other word and that indicates possession of the second word: my, your, his, her etc.
%\noindent cun: dog
%\noindent mó: my
%\noindent &gt; mó gun: my dog
%\noindent The possessive pronoun causes mutation on the following word in most cases, but not for the 3<sup>rd</sup> person singular feminine and not for the second person plural.
%\noindent mó: my
%\noindent tó: your
%\noindent ó: his
%\noindent ó: her
%\noindent nó: our
%\noindent só: your (pl.)
%\noindent só: their
%\noindent Because the third and the fourth and the sixth and the seven are the same, they are distinguished by the mutation: the third and the sixth cause mutation, the fourth and the seventh don’t.
%\noindent mó (my): mut.
%\noindent tó (your): mut.
%\noindent ó (his): mut.
%\noindent ó (her): <strong>no</strong> mut.
%\noindent nó (our): mut.
%\noindent só (your pl.): <strong>no</strong> mut.
%\noindent só (their): mut.
%\noindent mó <strong>g</strong>un: my dog
%\noindent tó <strong>g</strong>un: your dog
%\noindent ó <strong>g</strong>un: his dog
%\noindent ó <strong>c</strong>un: her dog
%\noindent nó <strong>g</strong>un: our dog
%\noindent só <strong>c</strong>un: your (pl.) dog
%\noindent só <strong>g</strong>un: their dog
%\noindent Remember that possession can also be expressed with the particle i-. In that case it uses the object pronouns, and they follow after the possessed thing.
%\noindent cun imí: a dog of mine
%\noindent cun ithí: a dog of yours
%\noindent cun iché: a dog of his
%\noindent cun ichí: a dog of hers
%\noindent cun iní: a dog of ours
%\noindent cun isú: a dog of yours (pl.)
%\noindent cun ichís: a dog of theirs

%<li><strong> Demonstratives</strong></li>
%</ol>
%\noindent A demonstrative is a word that indicates another word: this, that, those, these.
%\noindent In Galáthach the demonstratives have two parts.
%<ol>
%<li>the article “in” before the word</li>
%<li>the words –sin or –sé hyphenated to the end of the word</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent cun: dog
%\noindent &gt; in cun-sin: this dog
%\noindent &gt; in cun-sé: that dog
%\noindent If the word is feminine the article causes mutation.
%\noindent cunis: bitch
%\noindent &gt; in gunis-sin: this bitch
%\noindent &gt; in gunis-sé: that bitch
%\noindent If the word is plural the article and the demonstratives stay the same.
%\noindent in cúné-sin: these dogs
%\noindent in gunísé-sé: those bitches

%<li><strong> Locatives</strong></li>
%</ol>
%\noindent Locatives are words that indicate a position: here, there.
%\noindent There are two locatives in Galáthach.
%\noindent insin: here
%\noindent insé: there
%\noindent Esi in cun insin: the dog is here
%\noindent Esi in gunis insé: the bitch is there
%\noindent Esi in cun-sin insin: this dog is here
%\noindent Esi in gunis-sé insé: that bitch is there
