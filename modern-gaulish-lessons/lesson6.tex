\section{Menghavan 6: An\'{u}\'{e} Benin Can in hAmosanal \textendash\ Gwerthan\'{a}l\'{e} Coswaus Anolsam}
(\textit{Lesson 6: Feminine Nouns With The Article \textendash\ Initial Consonant Mutations})\\
%\noindent In the sixth lesson you will learn what happens to feminine nouns after the article.
%\noindent <strong>Feminine Nouns And The Article</strong>
%\noindent When feminine nouns are preceded by the article “in” their initial consonant changes according to regular patterns. This change is what marks the nouns as feminine.
%\noindent par &gt; in bar (cauldron – the cauldron)
%\noindent taran &gt; in daran (storm – the storm)
%\noindent carch &gt; in garch (rock – the rock)
%\noindent ben &gt; in ven (woman – the woman)
%\noindent dúithir &gt; in dhúithir (daughter – the daughter)
%\noindent glan &gt; in ghlan (river bank – the river bank)
%\noindent máthir &gt; in wáthir (mother – the mother)
%\noindent sir &gt; in shir (star – the star)
%\noindent spáthl &gt; in ‘páthl (story – the story)
%\noindent nath &gt; in nhath (fate – the fate)
%\noindent rath &gt; in rhath (fern – the fern)
%\noindent latha &gt; in latha (swamp – the swamp)
%\noindent fich &gt; in fhich (fig – the fig)
%\noindent The semi-vowel i- (/y/ in English, [j]) is prefixed with a ch’:
%\noindent iar &gt; in ch’iar (chicken – the chicken)
%\noindent Vowels are prefixed with a h-:
%\noindent aval &gt; in haval (apple – the apple)
%\noindent épis &gt; in hépis (mare – the mare)
%\noindent úlidh &gt; in húlidh (feast – the feast)
%\noindent ídh &gt; in hídh (chain – the chain)
%\noindent oghran &gt; in hoghran (frost – the frost)
%\noindent If the nouns are in the plural exactly the same thing happens:
%\noindent páré &gt; in báré
%\noindent taráné &gt; in daráné
%\noindent carché &gt; in garché
%\noindent mná &gt; in wná
%\noindent dúithíré &gt; in dhúithíré
%\noindent gláné &gt; in ghláné
%\noindent <strong>Initial Consonant Mutation Changes</strong>
%\noindent The changes shown above are regular, they happen like that all the time. They are summarised below:
%\noindent p &gt; b
%\noindent t &gt; d
%\noindent c &gt; g
%\noindent b &gt; v
%\noindent d &gt; dh
%\noindent g &gt; gh
%\noindent m &gt; w
%\noindent s &gt; sh if followed by a wowel
%\noindent s &gt; ‘ [nothing] if followed by a consonant
%\noindent n &gt; nh
%\noindent r &gt; rh
%\noindent l &gt; lh
%\noindent f &gt; fh
%\noindent i- &gt; ch’i- if followed by a vowel
%\noindent i- &gt; hi- if followed by a consonant
%\noindent a &gt; ha
%\noindent e &gt; he
%\noindent u &gt; hu
%\noindent o &gt; ho
%\noindent Some of these sounds are unusual.
%\noindent dh = as in English <strong><u>th</u></strong>e, <strong><u>th</u></strong>ere
%\noindent gh = as in Greek ?<strong><u>?</u></strong>? (I, me), Breton del<strong><u>c’h</u></strong> (to hold). The sound is called “voiced velar
%\noindent fricative”. Like a Scottish –ch (as in lo<strong>ch</strong>) but said with a “thick voice” (i.e. voiced).
%\noindent sh = as in English <strong><u>sh</u></strong>ip
%\noindent h- = as in English <strong><u>h</u></strong>ouse
%\noindent ch’ = as in Scottish lo<strong><u>ch</u></strong>
%\noindent nh = ch- as in Scottish lo<strong><u>ch, </u></strong>followed by –n &gt; sounds like chn-.
%\noindent rh = ch- as in Scottish lo<strong><u>ch, </u></strong>followed by –r &gt; sounds like chr-.
%\noindent lh = ch- as in Scottish lo<strong><u>ch, </u></strong>followed by –l &gt; sounds like chl-.
%\noindent fh = an /f/ produced without the tongue touching the teeth. The sound is called “voiceless bilabial fricative”.
%\noindent Of these sounds h-, sh-, fh-, nh-, rh- and lh- only ever occur as initial consonant mutations.
