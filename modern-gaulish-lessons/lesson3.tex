\subsection{Nouns}

\noindent The word ``noun'' means ``name''. It is a word that refers to anything that can have a name, such as a person, place, thing, state or quality. In lesson 1 and 2 we learned about subjects and objects. Nouns are things that can be subjects or objects of a sentence.\\
%\noindent e.g.:
%\noindent gwir: man
%\noindent cun: dog
%\noindent ép: horse
%\noindent cánu: song
%\noindent ménu: thought
%\noindent coch: leg
%\noindent duvr: water
%\noindent pen: head
%\noindent There is no indefinite article like English “a, an” in modern Gaulish:
%\noindent gwir: man
%\noindent gwir: a man
%\noindent cun: dog
%\noindent cun: a dog

%<li><strong> Possession</strong></li>
%</ol>
%\noindent In lesson 2 we saw that when a pronoun was used as an object it had a special possession particle i-. This particle is not used with anything else, only with the pronoun. When we use a noun we just replace the pronoun and the particle with a noun:
%\noindent cána mi cánu: I sing a song
%\noindent gwéla mi can ichí: I want to sing it
%\noindent gwéla mi can cánu: I want to sing a song
%\noindent The phrase “can cánu” means “[the] singing of a song”. The English word [the] is not used.
%\noindent This phrase has two nouns: 1. the verbal noun “can”. 2. the noun “cánu”. In this phrase the first noun “can” is possessed by the second noun “cánu”. In English this is indicated by the word “of”. In modern Gaulish this is indicated by the position of the word: the second word possesses the first word.
%\noindent The same can be done with any two nouns:
%\noindent curu gwir: a beer of a man [a man’s beer]
%\noindent gnath ben: a child of a woman [a woman’s child]
%\noindent ép geneth: a horse of a girl [a girl’s horse]

%<ol start="3">
%<li><strong> The Article</strong></li>
%</ol>
%\noindent Modern Gaulish has one article: “in”. It does not change for any reason.
%\noindent e.g.:
%\noindent in gwir: the man
%\noindent in ép: the horse
%\noindent in mapath: the boy
%\noindent in curu: the beer
%\noindent in pen: the head
%\noindent in duvr: the water
%\noindent The article “in” can be used in cases of possession. It can only be used with the second noun, which is the one possessing the first noun. The first noun can never have the article in front of it.
%\noindent e.g.:
%\noindent cun gwir: a dog of a man [a man’s dog]
%\noindent cun in gwir: a dog of the man [the man’s dog]
%\noindent The English phrase between brackets [&#8230;] shows a very good translation of the modern Gaulish phrase. It only uses one article and can only ever use one article. It is not possible to say “the man’s the dog”.
%\noindent The second noun possesses the first noun. The second noun is the only noun that can have the article.
