%\noindent 
%\noindent <strong>2. Question Formation</strong>
%\noindent 
%\noindent As you can see above questions are made by putting the question particle “a” in front of the verb in the sentence.
%\noindent 
%\noindent gnía ti: you know &gt; statement.
%\noindent a ghnía ti?: do you know? &gt; question.
%\noindent 
%\noindent The word order doesn’t change. The only difference is the question particle “a”. It causes an initial consonant mutation (ICM) on the following word.
%\noindent 
%\noindent gála mi: I can &gt; statement.
%\noindent a ghála mi?: can I? &gt; question.
%\noindent 
%\noindent <strong>Question Words</strong>
%\noindent 
%\noindent pé: what, which
%\noindent pé gaman: how
%\noindent pí: who
%\noindent péri: why
%\noindent pémái: where
%\noindent ponch: when
%\noindent 
%\noindent <strong>3. The Verb To Have</strong>
%\noindent 
%\noindent Galáthach does not have a specific verb “to have”. Instead it uses a compound construction using the verb “to be”. It is made of the personal pronoun of the entity that “has” something, followed by the verb “to be”. The two are connected with a hyphen.
%\noindent 
%\noindent mi-esi ép: I have a horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent &gt; mi-esi translates as “with-me, to-me” &gt; mi-esi ép = “to-me” is a horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent The conjugation is as follows:
%\noindent 
%\noindent mi-esi: I have
%\noindent ti-esi: you have
%\noindent é-esi: he has
%\noindent í-esi: she has
%\noindent ni-esi: we have
%\noindent sú-esi: you (pl.)
%\noindent sí-esi: they have
%\noindent 
%\noindent If the subject of a phrase is specified it comes first, followed by the pronoun+to-be construction.
%\noindent 
%\noindent in gwir: the man
%\noindent é-esi: he has
%\noindent cun: dog
%\noindent &gt; in gwir é-esi cun: the man has a dog
%\noindent 
%\noindent in dóné: the people
%\noindent sí-esi: they have
%\noindent gavré: goats
%\noindent &gt; in dóné sí-esi gavré: the people have goats
%\noindent 
%\noindent In a question the question particle “a” comes before the pronoun+to-be construction. It causes mutation to the first letter of the construction.
%\noindent 
%\noindent pé a di-esi: what do you have?
%\noindent péri a shú-esi épé: why do you (pl.) have horses?
%\noindent 
%\noindent In a question where the subject is specified the question particle comes between the subject and the pronoun+to-be construction.
%\noindent 
%\noindent péri in gwir a hé-esi cun: why does the man have a dog?
%\noindent péri in dóné a shí-esi gavré: why do the people have goats?

%\noindent <strong>4. Subordinate clauses</strong>
%\noindent 
%\noindent A subordinate clause is a sentence inside of another sentence. It has a verb, subject and object that are independent of the main sentence. The two are linked with the particle “o”, which translates in English as “that” or “which”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent gnía ti: you know
%\noindent o: that
%\noindent ti-esi: you have
%\noindent pen: a head
%\noindent tech: beautiful
%\noindent &gt; gnía ti <strong><u>o</u></strong> ti-esi pen tech: you know <strong><u>that </u></strong>you have a beautiful head
%\noindent 
%\noindent apísa mi: I see
%\noindent o: that
%\noindent né hesi: there is not
%\noindent curu: beer
%\noindent éth: more
%\noindent &gt; apísa mi <strong><u>o</u></strong> né hesi curu éth: I see <strong><u>that</u></strong> there is no more beer
%\noindent 
%\noindent In the subordinate clause the second sentence (“there is no more beer”) can stand alone independently from the first one (“I see”). The two are connected by the particle “o”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent If the particle “o”is followed by a word that starts with a vowel it becomes “och”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent gwídha mi: I understand
%\noindent o: that
%\noindent esi ti: you are
%\noindent lisc: tired
%\noindent &gt; gwídha mi <strong><u>och</u></strong> esi ti lisc: I understand <strong><u>that</u></strong> you are tired
