%\noindent (Tarchonwoth: Good day, how are you?)

%\noindent Tuthchána: Dí wath adhith cóéth. Esi mi in dhái, bráthu. Ach ti-súé?
%\noindent (Tuthchana: Good day to you too. I am well, thank you. And yourself?)

%\noindent Tuthchána: Gnía mi. Gnía mi ben shen o gála ó dúithir rinóthi ép adhith. Esi ó anu Cunvára. Ái a gantha hal in bron ach pétha adhí insé.

%\noindent (Tarchonwoth: Thanks very much, you’re very helpful.)
%\noindent

%\noindent (Tuthchana: It’s my pleasure. It’s nothing.)

%\noindent (Cinvara: It is myself. Do you want to see the horse that my daughter can sell?)
%\noindent 
%\noindent Tarchonwoth: In chwír, gwéla mi.
%\noindent (Tarchonwoth: Yes, I do.)
%\noindent

%\noindent (Cunvára: It is there, look. Does it please you?)

%<ol>
%<li><strong>Direct Clauses</strong></li>
%</ol>
%\noindent 
%\noindent A direct clause is a part of a sentence that refers back to the subject of the previous part of the sentence.
%\noindent 
%\noindent &gt; this is the man who wants to buy a horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent The direct clause is “who wants to buy a horse”. The word “who” refers to “the man”, which was the subject of the previous part of the sentence “this is the man”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent In Galáthach there are four ways of constructing this sentence:
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>a) introducing the clause with the word “o” and not restating the subject. The word “o” means “that” or “which”. If it is followed by a word that starts with a vowel it becomes “och”.</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent 
%\noindent esi: is
%\noindent sin: this
%\noindent in gwir: the man
%\noindent o: that (which)
%\noindent gwel: to want
%\noindent prin: to buy
%\noindent in ép: the horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent &gt; esi sin in gwir o gwéla prin in ép: this is the man who wants to buy the horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent The word “o” connects the two phrases and refers to “the man”. There is no confusion because there is no other word in the second phrase that can be interpreted as a subject:
%\noindent 
%\noindent gwéla prin in ép: want buying the horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent The word “ép” can not be interpreted as a subject because it does not follow the verb immediately. In this case there is no need to restate the subject.
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>b) introducing the clause with the word “o” and restating the subject. This is necessary when there can be confusion about which is the subject of the second phrase.</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent 
%\noindent esi sin in gwir o gwéla in ép &gt; this sentence can be interpreted in two ways:
%\noindent 
%\noindent &#8211; this is the man who wants the horse
%\noindent &#8211; this is the man who the horse wants
%\noindent 
%\noindent Because the words “in ép”, “the horse” follow directly after the verb they can be interpreted as being the subject of the second verb “gwel”, “wanting”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent Therefore the subject needs to be restated with a pronoun that refers back to the subject of the first phrase. This is called using a resumptive pronoun.
%\noindent 
%\noindent esi sin in gwir o gwéla é in ép: this is the man who wants the horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent The literal translation of this sentence is “this is the man that he wants the horse”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent In reality the direct clause becomes a subordinate clause linked to the main clause with the word “o”.
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>c) linking the second clause to the first clause with the suffix –ió which gets attached to the end of the verb of the second clause.</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent 
%\noindent esi sin in gwir gwelió in ép: this is the man who wants the horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent The suffix –ió always refers back to the subject immediately in front of it. The literal translation of this sentence is something like “this is the man wanting the horse”.
%\noindent 
%<ol>
%<li>d) linking the second clause to the first clause with the preposition “en” in front of the verb. This causes initial consonant mutation on the verb. The verb is in the root or infinitive form.</li>
%</ol>
%\noindent 
%\noindent esi sin in gwir en chwel in ép
%\noindent 
%\noindent The literal translation of this sentence is “this is the man wanting the horse”. It is the same as using the suffix –ió.
%\noindent 
%\noindent Because the verb is in the root or infinitive form there can be no confusion about what is the subject of the second clause, because a subject can never follow a verb in the root or infinitive form.
%\noindent 
%\noindent Using the preposition “en” to link a second clause to a main clause can only be done in the present tense.
%\noindent 
%\noindent 
%\noindent People choose freely from these four ways of constructing direct clauses. However, the second one (b) is the easiest one.
%\noindent 
%\noindent 
%<ol start="2">
%<li><strong> Indirect Clauses</strong></li>
%</ol>
%\noindent 
%\noindent A direct clause is a part of a sentence that refers back to the subject of the previous part of the sentence, but in an indirect way.
%\noindent 
%\noindent this is the man whose daughter wants to buy the horse
%\noindent 
%\noindent The second clause starts with “whose daughter”. The words “whose daughter” refer back to the subject of the previous clause, but the actual subject of the second clause is the daughter, not the man himself.
%\noindent 
%\noindent In Galáthach there is only one way to construct this sentence. The clause is introduced with the word “o” and the subject is stated independently. The subject becomes “his daughter”.
%\noindent 
%\noindent esi sin in gwir o gwéla ó dhúithir prin in ép
%\noindent 
%\noindent The literal translation of this sentence is “this is the man that his daughter wants to buy the horse”. As above in Direct Clauses b) the clause becomes a subordinate clause with its own independent subject, linked to the first clause with the word “o”.

%\noindent 
%\noindent That is the woman who talks to cats: Esi sé in ven o lavára can gáthé.
%\noindent Here is the man who is a bad singer: Insin esi in gwir och esi é caníath druch.
%\noindent This the child that eats sand: Esi sin in mapath o depra é grau
